CHAPTER 1 — THE CHANNEL AND THE MODEL
BinMoments — a listening companion

(Written to be listened to, not studied. No diagrams, no code — just the idea.)


Let me start with the problem the model is solving, because it's subtler than it first looks. You're building a system for sensor data, and the obvious assumption is that a sensor reading is a number. Temperature: twenty-two degrees. One number. And for temperature, that's true. But the moment you look at the full range of things instruments measure, that assumption cracks. Think about wind. Wind isn't one number — it has a speed and a direction. Ten kilometres an hour, from the northwest. That's not a number, it's an arrow: it has length and it points somewhere. And it gets richer still. Some physical quantities — things like the electrical conductivity of a material, or mechanical stress — aren't even arrows. They behave differently along different directions in space, so describing them takes a little grid of numbers, not one, not three, but a whole oriented block.

So the model has to face a question right at the foundation: how do you store sensor data when a "reading" might be a plain number, or an arrow, or an oriented block — without building three completely different systems, and without building some monstrous general-purpose thing that tries to handle everything and never gets finished?

The answer the model lands on is an idea worth savoring, because it's elegant. Every reading, no matter how complicated, gets split into two parts: a magnitude and a direction. The magnitude is "how much" — a single number. The direction is "which way" — and for a plain temperature, there simply isn't one, so it's empty. For wind, the magnitude is the speed, the direction is the bearing. For that oriented block, the magnitude is an overall size and the direction is the orientation of the block in space. The trick is that no matter what came in, you always pull out one clean number — the magnitude — and you set the direction aside.

Why does pulling out one clean number matter so much? Because everything the rest of the system does — all the statistics, all the anomaly detection — works on single numbers. You can build a histogram of single numbers. You can average single numbers, measure how spread out they are, ask whether the distribution is drifting. You cannot do any of that cleanly on arrows or oriented blocks — the maths gets tangled fast. So the model acts like a doorway: complicated, rich data arrives at the door, and what passes through into the engine room is always a tidy stream of single numbers. The richness isn't thrown away — the direction is kept off to the side, in case you want it later — but it doesn't clog up the statistical machinery. In the code, that doorway is one small function whose entire job is: take whatever came in, hand back the magnitude and the direction. For temperature it does almost nothing — the number is already the magnitude. But the seam is there, so that the day you add wind, nothing downstream has to change. The wind speed flows through the exact same pipes the temperature did.

Now, there's a second concept living in the model, and it's the idea of a channel. A channel is the description of one measurable thing an instrument produces. It carries a name — "temperature." It carries what kind of object it is — a plain number, an arrow, a block. It carries its unit — degrees Celsius. And it carries one more flag that's quietly important: is this quantity linear or circular? That sounds abstract, so let me make it concrete, because it's a lovely little trap. Most quantities are linear — they live on a straight line. Cold to hot, low to high. But direction is circular — it lives on a compass. And on a compass, three hundred and fifty degrees and ten degrees are right next to each other, only twenty degrees apart, even though as plain numbers they look three hundred and forty apart. So if you naively averaged a bunch of wind directions clustered around due north, you'd get a nonsensical answer pointing due south, because the numbers wrap around and ordinary arithmetic doesn't know that. The model carries a little flag saying "this channel is circular, handle it with circular maths," so that later, when wind arrives, the system doesn't fall into that trap. For temperature the flag just says "linear," and life is simple — but the flag is there, anticipating the harder case.

Now here's the part I want you to notice, because it's the discipline of the whole project showing up in miniature. The model understands all three cases — number, arrow, block. It has the design for all three written down. But it only builds two of them: the plain number and the arrow. The third, the oriented block, is deliberately left unbuilt. If a reading of that type ever comes through, the code doesn't fake it — it stops and says, in effect, "this is designed but not yet built; here's the note explaining why." That's not laziness. The oriented-block case has genuine open questions that can only be answered well when you have a real instrument of that kind in front of you, and building it now, guessing, on pretend data, would mean baking in the wrong guess. So the model knows how to handle it, has reasoned it through, and consciously waits. You get full credit for understanding the hard case without paying the cost of building something premature.

There's one more quiet decision in the measurement record itself, and it echoes something you already liked in the simulator. A measurement carries which instrument it came from, the moment it's about, its values, and an optional tag used later if a reading ever needs correcting. But notice what it deliberately does not carry: the time the system received it. That receiving-time is stamped on later, when the data actually arrives at the front door. The reading is about one moment; the system learns about it at another moment; and the model keeps those two moments separate from the very first data structure — because, as you'll see when we get there, the ability to ask "what did I know, and when did I know it" depends entirely on never muddling those two times together.

So that's the model: a doorway that turns any reading, however rich, into a clean stream of single numbers for the engine to chew on, while quietly preserving the richness and the awkward special cases off to the side. Plain numbers and arrows are built; the oriented block is designed and waiting; the circular-versus-linear flag is set so the compass trap is sprung in advance; and the two kinds of time are kept apart from the start. Small module, but it's the foundation the whole tower stands on — which is exactly why we built it first.

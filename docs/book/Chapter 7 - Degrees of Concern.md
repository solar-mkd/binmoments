# Chapter 7 — Degrees of Concern

*BinMoments — a listening companion. Written to be listened to, not studied. No diagrams, no code — just the idea.*


## Two different questions

The drift detector from the last chapter answers one question beautifully: *has this instrument changed from its own normal?* But notice what that question quietly assumes — that normal is fine. Drift is a comparison to the instrument's own past, so it only ever tells you about *change*. And change isn't the only thing worth worrying about. Imagine a temperature sensor that has been reading dangerously high for a year, steadily, every summer afternoon. It has no drift — nothing is changing, it's been doing this all along — so the drift detector stays silent. And yet the readings are dangerous. Drift would shrug; something is still wrong.

So there's a second, completely different question the system needs to answer, and it's almost the opposite of the first: *never mind whether it changed — are the values themselves in a range we should care about, right now, in absolute terms?* This is not a question about an instrument's personal history. It's a question about the world. Forty-two degrees is hot whether or not the sensor has ever read forty-two before. These two questions — has it changed, and is it dangerous — are independent. A sensor can drift while staying perfectly safe, or sit rock-steady in a hazardous range. You want both questions answered, because each catches what the other misses.

## Bands: naming the ranges that matter

The way the system answers the second question is with bands — named ranges along the value axis, drawn where they mean something in the real world. For temperature you might say: everything up to twenty-eight degrees is Nominal, twenty-eight to thirty-three is Elevated, thirty-three to thirty-eight is Warning, and anything above thirty-eight is Critical. Four labelled zones, with fixed boundaries. Where exactly you put those boundaries is a domain decision — it depends on what the instrument is for, what's safe, what's alarming — and that's precisely why they're set deliberately rather than learned from data. Unlike the buckets, which discovered their edges from a year of history, the bands are *told* their edges by someone who knows what the numbers mean. A meteorologist, an engineer, an operator. The bands encode human judgment about consequence, not statistics about frequency.

And here's the satisfying part — the bands cost almost nothing to compute, because they ride on top of the distribution the system already holds. Remember every hour is already summarized as a little distribution across buckets. To find out how much of that hour fell into each band, you don't go back to the raw readings — you read it straight off the buckets. You walk across the distribution, and for each band you add up the share of mass that landed in its range. A bucket that happens to straddle a boundary gets split between the two bands it overlaps, in proportion. And mass that poured out past the very top — readings beyond anything ever seen — counts toward the most severe band, because a reading off the top of the scale is, if anything, the most concerning of all. The result is a tidy one-line verdict for the hour: ninety-six percent Nominal, four percent Warning, say. Or, on a bad day, mostly Critical.

## Seeing it in action

The contrast tells the whole story. Take a normal summer afternoon: most of its readings sit in Nominal, a good chunk in Elevated because summer afternoons are genuinely warm, a sliver creeping into Warning, and nothing Critical. That's a healthy fingerprint for the season. Now take a day where something has gone badly wrong — a fault that drives the temperature twelve degrees too high. The same readout flips dramatically: nothing left in Nominal at all, a little Elevated, a large slab of Warning, and the majority sitting in Critical. You don't need to inspect a single raw number to see the day has gone wrong; the band occupancy says it at a glance, in language a human immediately understands. And crucially, this works alongside the drift detector, not instead of it. Drift would also flag that twelve-degree fault, because it's a big change. But the bands add something drift can't: they say not just *that* it changed, but *how serious the result is* — that the values have crossed into the range we actually care about. One signal says "different"; the other says "dangerous." Together they paint a fuller picture than either alone.

## Knowing when to stop

There's a temptation here that's worth resisting, and resisting it is part of the design. Once you have bands, you can imagine endless cleverness layered on top. Don't just flag a Critical reading — flag it only if it stays Critical for ten minutes. Or only if it's Critical *and* drifting. Or rising at more than a certain rate. Or Critical at night but not by day. Each of these is reasonable; together they're a whole rule engine, an open-ended language for composing conditions. And it would be easy to start building it, because every individual rule sounds useful.

But the system deliberately stops at the simple, static bands — the foundation — and leaves the general rule engine for later, clearly marked as a future possibility rather than a half-built feature. This is the same discipline that runs through the whole project: draw a line, build the solid thing on this side of it, and write down the ideas on the far side so they're captured but not chased. The static bands are genuinely useful on their own, and they're the bedrock any future rule engine would stand on. Building that engine now, before there's a real operator with real rules to express, would mean guessing at a language nobody has asked for yet — exactly the kind of speculative complexity that keeps a project from ever finishing. So the bands are complete, and the engine waits, by choice.

That's value-band classification: a second, independent signal that asks not whether an instrument has changed but whether its readings have entered a range that matters; bands drawn by human judgment rather than learned from data; occupancy read cheaply off the distribution the system already keeps; a plain-language verdict for each hour that complements the drift signal; and a deliberate stopping point that leaves the elaborate rule engine as a captured idea rather than an unfinished build.

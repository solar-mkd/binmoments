# Chapter 3 — Binning

*BinMoments — a listening companion. Written to be listened to, not studied. No diagrams, no code — just the idea.*


## The problem: too many numbers

By now the system has a clean stream of single numbers flowing in — temperatures, one after another, thousands a day, millions over a year. And here's the first real question of the statistical core: what do you actually keep? You can't store every reading forever and re-scan all of them every time someone asks a question — that doesn't scale, and it's not even what you want. What you want is a compact summary that captures the *shape* of the data: not "here are all eight thousand readings," but "here's what the spread of temperatures looked like." A fingerprint of the distribution. And the classic tool for that is the histogram — you divide the range of possible values into buckets, and for each bucket you just keep a count of how many readings fell into it. Instead of eight thousand numbers, you keep a few dozen counts. That's binning: turning a flood of readings into a small, faithful summary of their distribution.

## The obvious way, and why it's wrong

Now, the obvious way to build a histogram is to chop the range into equal slices. Temperature runs from, say, six degrees to thirty-five — so cut that into bins of equal width, maybe one degree each, and count. Simple. And wrong for what we need, in a way that's worth understanding, because the flaw is subtle and it drives the whole design.

Think about where temperature actually spends its time. In a place like Brisbane, the vast majority of readings cluster in a fairly narrow band — most hours sit somewhere in the low twenties. The extremes — the very cold winter dawns, the rare scorching afternoons — happen seldom. So if you use equal-width bins, something lopsided happens. The bins in the crowded middle are packed with thousands of readings — but they're coarse, each one degree wide, so they blur together exactly the region where all the interesting detail lives. Meanwhile the bins out in the tails are nearly empty, a handful of readings scattered across them — wasted resolution where there's almost nothing to resolve. You've spent your detail in the wrong place. You have fine buckets where you don't need them and coarse buckets where you do. That's backwards.

## The idea: equal mass, not equal width

So here's the move the project makes, and it's the heart of the whole thing. Instead of making the bins equal in *width*, make them equal in *mass* — equal in how many readings each one holds. Let the bin boundaries fall wherever they need to so that every bucket ends up with the same number of readings. The consequence is beautiful: where the data is dense, in that crowded band around twenty-one degrees, the bins automatically become narrow — because it doesn't take much width to gather a bucketful when readings are packed tight. And out in the sparse tails, the bins automatically become wide — because you have to reach across a lot of degrees to collect the same bucketful when readings are scarce.

I find it helps to picture the actual numbers. With sixteen equal-mass bins fitted to a year of Brisbane temperature, the bins down in the busy middle come out under one degree wide each — fine, sharp resolution right where every reading lives. And the bins at the cold and hot extremes come out five or six degrees wide — coarse, but that's fine, because almost nothing is out there. Every bin holds the same count, but they cover wildly different spans. The resolution went, all by itself, exactly where the data is. You didn't have to hand-tune it; you just asked for equal mass, and the data placed the boundaries for you.

And this isn't a cosmetic preference — it's what makes the later questions answerable. When you eventually want to ask "what's the ninety-fifth percentile, the value that only five percent of readings exceed," that answer lives way out in the upper tail. With equal-width bins, the tail is a near-empty blur and you can't pin the percentile down. With equal-mass bins, even the tail bins hold a solid bucketful, so the tail is properly resolved and the percentile is trustworthy. The design choice you made for the middle pays off in the tails too.

## Where the edges come from

So how does the system decide where to put those boundaries? It learns them from history — from at least a full year of past readings. A year, because temperature breathes with the seasons, and you need to have seen a whole cycle — the coldest winter dawn and the hottest summer afternoon — before you can honestly say where this instrument's values live. The system takes that year of readings, sorts them, and finds the cut points that divide them into equal-sized groups. Those cut points become the bin edges. The edges are not chosen by a human guessing; they're discovered from the data itself.

And there's a quiet but important distinction hiding here, two separate decisions that are easy to muddle. One decision is *where* the boundaries go — and that's driven entirely by the shape of the data, by where the readings actually fell. The other decision is *how many* bins to have at all — and that's driven by something completely different: how many readings you'll have in each comparison you want to make. More bins means finer detail, but it also means fewer readings per bin, which makes each count noisier and less reliable. So you only earn the right to lots of bins if you have lots of readings to fill them. A single hour might only have a few dozen readings — too few for fine bins — so fine-grained comparison waits until you pool a week's worth, where the counts are dense enough to support the detail. Resolution is cheap to ask for, but it only pays off where the counts justify it. The system keeps those two questions — where the edges go, how many there are — firmly separate, because they answer to different masters.

## The edges of the edges: overflow

Now, what about a reading that falls outside everything the system has ever seen — colder than the coldest, hotter than the hottest in that whole training year? The bins cover the observed range, from the historical minimum to the historical maximum. Beyond each end sits a special catch-all: an underflow bin for anything below the lowest ever seen, an overflow bin for anything above the highest. And here's why that's deliberately anchored to the actual extremes rather than to some percentage cutoff: it means a reading landing in overflow has a clear, strong meaning — *this instrument just produced a value beyond anything it recorded in a year*. That's not noise; that's news. It's exactly the kind of genuinely novel event the whole system exists to notice. We saw it work: a fifty-eight-degree spike, dropped into a year of Brisbane data, lands straight in the overflow bin, flagged as the impossible reading it is. If the boundary had been drawn at some ordinary percentile instead, normal readings would spill into overflow all the time and the signal would be meaningless. Anchored to the true extreme, overflow stays quiet until something real happens.

## Freezing the ruler

There's one more idea, and it's about discipline. The moment you derive those bin edges from data, you've created something that has to be treated with care — because the edges are now a kind of ruler, and you can only compare measurements taken with the *same* ruler. If you counted last month's readings using one set of edges and this month's using a different set, the two sets of counts mean different things and adding them together is nonsense. So once a set of edges is derived, it's *frozen*, and it's stamped with an identity — a short fingerprint computed from the edges themselves. This gives two lovely properties. Derive the schema twice from the same year of data, and you get the exact same fingerprint — fully reproducible. But re-derive it a year later on a fresh year of data — because the climate has shifted, the instrument has aged, the edges have moved a little — and you automatically get a *different* fingerprint, marking it as a new, distinct ruler. The system can then be strict about never mixing counts from two different rulers, just by checking whether their fingerprints match. The identity isn't decoration; it's the safety latch that keeps the whole counting scheme honest.

That's binning. A flood of single numbers becomes a compact distribution fingerprint; the buckets carry equal mass so resolution follows the data, sharp where it's crowded and coarse where it's empty; the edges are learned from a year of history; anything beyond the historical extremes is caught and flagged as novel; and the whole ruler is frozen and fingerprinted so comparisons stay honest. The bins are half the project's name, and now you can see why — they're the foundation for the shape of the distribution: the percentiles, the entropy, and the drift signal that compares one distribution against another. (There's one thing the bins turn out to be the wrong tool for — the simple averages and spreads, the moments — and a later chapter tells the story of how building the system revealed that, and where those numbers come from instead. The bins shine at shape; something else handles the moments.)
